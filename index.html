東京 · Asakusa, Tokyo

The Gate at Dusk

The smell of incense reaches you before anything else — thick, sweet, older than the street noise behind you. You have been here before, in photographs. But standing under Kaminarimon, you realize you never knew what it felt like. A bell tolls once. Somewhere ahead, people move in quiet ritual, and you've forgotten everything the guidebook said.

628 AD · Sumida River

Pulled from the River

Two brothers cast their nets and drew up a small golden figure — the Bodhisattva Kannon, barely the size of a palm. They returned her to the water. She returned to their nets. Three times. They understood. The temple you're standing in was built around that refusal to be ignored — and it has never stopped collecting pilgrims since.

Now · The Incense Urn

Hands in the Smoke

An elderly woman draws smoke toward her face with cupped hands — unhurried, practiced, as if washing. A child beside her copies the gesture, uncertain but earnest. Neither speaks. The ritual doesn't need explanation. You already know, standing there, that you want to remember this moment.

End of Experience

Stories don't leave unless we carry them out.

Most of us walk through extraordinary places and forget them by dinner.
But a story — short, felt, chosen — stays in the body longer than a photo does.

Design Notes

  • Short segments — Each scene is under 3 sentences. Constraint forces precision; readers don't skim what's already brief.
  • Choices create investment — Deciding between paths makes the story feel personal, not broadcast. You chose this version.
  • Context anchors memory — When you read "Sumida River, 628 AD" while standing near Senso-ji, it becomes a real coordinate, not trivia.
  • Guided freedom tradeoff — One fork keeps scope tight while preserving a sense of agency. More branches = more richness, more maintenance.
